# Infant Vaccines: Birth Through 12 Months


This guide summarizes the routine vaccines commonly recommended for infants from birth to 1 year in the United States. Exact timing can vary by product, health history, and whether a baby is catching up on missed doses. [web:5][web:7]

## Vaccines by age

| Age | Recommended vaccines |
|---|---|
| Birth | Hepatitis B (HepB). Some infants may also receive RSV preventive antibody protection depending on season and risk. [web:7] |
| 2 months | DTaP, Hib, IPV, PCV, Rotavirus, and the next HepB dose if using the standard series timing. [web:3][web:7] |
| 4 months | DTaP, Hib, IPV, PCV, Rotavirus. [web:3][web:7] |
| 6 months | DTaP, Hib, IPV, PCV, Rotavirus if the product requires a 3-dose series, and the annual flu vaccine begins at 6 months. [web:3][web:7] |
| 6–11 months | Flu vaccine yearly starting at 6 months; COVID-19 vaccine may also be recommended starting at 6 months depending on current guidance and product availability. [web:3] |
| 12 months | MMR, Varicella, and Hepatitis A typically begin at or after the first birthday. [web:4][web:6] |

## Vaccine list

- Hepatitis B protects against a liver infection that can cause long-term damage. [web:4][web:7]
- DTaP protects against diphtheria, tetanus, and pertussis, also called whooping cough. [web:3][web:7]
- Hib protects against invasive infections such as meningitis and bloodstream infection. [web:3][web:4]
- IPV protects against polio. [web:4][web:7]
- PCV protects against pneumococcal disease, including pneumonia, bloodstream infection, and meningitis. [web:4][web:7]
- Rotavirus protects against severe diarrhea and dehydration. [web:4][web:7]
- Flu vaccine starts at 6 months and is given every year. [web:3][web:7]
- MMR, Varicella, and Hepatitis A usually begin at 12 months. [web:4][web:6]

## Notes for parents

Some babies need a different schedule if they were born early, are catching up after missed doses, or have certain medical conditions. [web:5][web:7]  
RSV protection for infants may be given as an antibody product rather than a vaccine, depending on age, season, and risk factors. [web:7]  
A pediatrician can confirm the exact timing and which products are used for each dose. [web:5][web:8]
